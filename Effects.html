The Impact of Cancel Culture on Freedom of Expression

The Encyclopaedia Britannica describes cancel culture as the removal of support for individuals and their work due to a diverse opinion or action committed that was deemed as objectionable to the parties that are calling the out in public (Millet, 2023). Furthermore, social media is the platform used as a way to magnify public knowledge of the perceived offence from which the cancel campaign spreads (Millet, 2023). Philosopher, Norbert Bileny, points out that cancelling is a practice which is repeated and has taken different forms throughout history (Millet, 2023). On the other hand, cancel culture supporters argue that the practice is a means of empowering minorities, a way for people with less power than others to demand accountability when the system has failed to bring the offender to justice. In addition, the practice of cancelling is also a means to bring about social change and exercise the legitimate right to criticise and ask questions (Millet, 2023).

For critics of cancel culture, asking questions runs the risk of turning into harassment. They make the point that cancelling leads to bullying and can incite violence and threats worse than the original offence in question (Millet, 2023). Furthermore, it can also turn the internet into an uncontrolled courtroom rather than bringing social change (Millet, 2023).

In an editorial from an Australian newspaper known as The Age (2020) it was commented that many who take part in cancel culture, they believe that they have the right to determine what limitations should be enforced on free speech. Whenever someone crosses the line, punishment would quickly follow. This, unfortunately, is mob justice with no official adjudicator (Bromell, 2022).

Among the generations of Silents and Boomers, liberals were slightly more likely than conservatives to support the free speech rights of someone with racist views, however, by the Millenial birth years support among liberals began to decline (Twenge, 2023). Therefore, supporting free speech, even for someone with racist views, is now more common among conservatives. Support for shutting down speech goes beyond racism and sexism to include political views and can go fully cancel culture to include people losing their jobs (Twenge, 2023). As politics become more deeply embedded with moral cultures and emotions, tolerance for opposing viewpoints fades, with Millenials and Generation Z at the forefront (Twenge, 2023).

Controversies around free speech have divided older generations from younger ones for decades, however, now the political and generational sides have been reversed (Twenge, 2023). In the past, younger generations and liberals more often supported free speech rights, and older generations and conservatives more often opposed them (Twenge, 2023).
