What is Freedom of Expression?

The term "cancel culture" has both uncertain origins and meaning (McGarvey Hidy, 2021). However, the Pew Research Center had reported that the term's origin came from a 1980s song which used the word "cancel" as a term referring to a break up with someone (McGarvey Hidy, 2021). With its repeated use in television and film, the term spread to social media as well. It is the spurning of individuals and brands because of their comments, actions, or stances (Carlow University, 2022).

Cancel culture is presently commonly used in a pejorative sense within mainstream commentary. Media studies scholar Meredith Clark argued that cancelling and related practices such as "calling out" have been problematically "counter- framed through application of the reductive and malignant label of 'cancel culture'" (Ng, 2022). Furthermore, cancel culture is used as a common way for people to speak against something they perceive as wrong. These public call-outs typically start on digital media.

People calling out perceived offences and injustices made by an individual or organisation is not new. Social media is particularly damaging because of its broad reach and ability to diminish the human emotions of the one being cancelled (Carlow University, 2022). Therefore, it leaves a digital trail of what could have been a momentary slip and thus, leaving the individual or organisation vulnerable to any future insults (Carlow University, 2022).

Social media platforms are central spaces where relevant content is posted. It is also a space where public figures and fans interact with 'proof' of wrongdoing often drawn from public and private social media posts (Ng, 2022). One specific example of popular media cancelling is the case of beauty vlogger James Charles in 2019, in which fans judge other celebrities for their misbehaviour towards other people around them, generally also celebrities, in instances of interpersonal "drama" (Ng, 2022). Another kind of cancelling includes fan activism, such as the fan cancelling of the television series The 100 in 2016 due to issues of LGBTQ representation and the 2014 #CancelColbert campaign spurred by a problematic tweet in its references to Asians (Ng, 2022).

The high-risk of cancel culture comes from it being anonymous, especially with our tendency to find more courage to say things through social media or other digital platforms that we would not say face to face to another individual (Carlow University, 2022 & Dudenhoefer, 2020). According to psychology experts, this type of pack mentality, also known, as mob mentality is the kind of "justice" which leaves people feeling ostracised and isolated from society. In fact, this brings up the core element of cancel culture, public shaming, which has been used since socities were formed (Dudenhoefer, 2020). Amanda Koontz builds on top of this and states that public shaming is public ritual that has been used for a long time which helped uphold social bonds and make sure that people within a community were equal and understood the norms (Dudenhoefer, 2020).
