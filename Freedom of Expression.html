What is Freedom of Expression?

Freedom of Expression and freedom of speech are often times used interchangeably. Freedom of speech is the architecture of a free society (Millet, 2023). Ones voice matter, individuals have a right to say what they think, share information, and demand a better world. We also have a right to agree or disagree with those in power and to express these opinions in peaceful protests (Amnesty International, 2023).

Schmon and Kuczerawy (2021) explain that in Europe, the right for freedom of expression is defined as a negative right that prohibits the state from unduly restricting expression, similar to the United States. However, unlike the United States, Europe the right extends to a positive obligation. That is, states are also required to protect this right to free expression from interference by others (Bromell, 2022).

In the 2020s, the free speech debate accelerated into cancel culture (Twenge, 2023). In its name, works, opinions, and even people are cancelled. Advocates of cancel culture contend that it is a way of giving a voice to overlooked minorities and of limiting unacceptable opinions and actions (Millet, 2023). Its critics, on the other hand, warn of the danger it poses to freedom of expression (Millet, 2023). Clashes over speech and viewpoints frequently become intergenerational warefare, often this is seen between Generation X and Millenials (Twenge, 2023).

These clashes over the freedom of expression came from the want for safe spaces rooted in a slower life, with young adults seeking childhood conforts. With this view, protection and comfort and given more importance than open discussion (Twenge, 2023). Twenge (2023) states that "Millenials, on the slower side of slow-life strategy, favor protection, while Gen X'ers' earlier path to adolescence favored toghness and thus more immunity to the idea that words are violence." Individualism which favours protecting those who have been historically discriminated, intertwines with the slow-life strategy of protection to tell Millenials who to protect and who to go against (Twenge, 2023). Therefore, these all lead to instant communication and mob mentality of the internet.

Bromell (2022) argues that in a free, open, and democratic society, "restrictions on freedom of expression should be lawful, necessary and proportionate." Therefore, this implies transparency, accountable, and opportunities for review and appeal, whether restrictions on freedom of expression should be exercised by private companies or public authorities (Bromell, 2022).
